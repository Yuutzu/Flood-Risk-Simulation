Example Explanation:
Modeling and Simulation is a technique used to represent and analyze real-world systems without disrupting actual operations. It is especially useful in service systems such as banks and cashier counters where customer waiting time and resource utilization are critical factors. This project focuses on simulating a bank/cashier queue system using discrete-event simulation to evaluate system performance and identify possible improvements.
2. Problem Description
This section clearly states the issue that motivated the study.
What to include:
•	Current problem situation
•	Effects of the problem
•	Why it needs analysis
Example Explanation:
In many bank and cashier service systems, customers experience long waiting times due to limited service counters and unpredictable arrival patterns. These delays can lead to customer dissatisfaction and inefficient use of resources. Management often finds it difficult to decide whether adding more cashiers or adjusting service times would improve performance. Therefore, a simulation approach is required to analyze the system behavior before making costly real-world changes.

4. System Description
This section explains how the real system works.
What to include:
•	Entities
•	Resources
•	Queues
•	Events
Example Explanation:
The system consists of customers arriving at a bank or cashier counter to receive service. Customers form a single waiting line if the cashier is busy. Once the cashier becomes available, the next customer in the queue is served. After service completion, the customer exits the system. The system operates during normal business hours and processes customers on a first-come, first-served basis.
5. Model Design
Model design explains how the system is represented in the simulation software (e.g., ARENA).
What to include:
•	Modules used
•	Flow of entities
•	Logical structure
Example Explanation:
The simulation model is designed using the following modules:
•	Create Module: Generates customer arrivals based on a specified inter arrival time distribution
•	Process Module: Represents the cashier service where customers seize the cashier resource, experience a service delay, and then release the resource
•	Dispose Module: Removes customers from the system after service completion
The entity flow follows the sequence: Create → Queue → Service → Exit.
6. Assumptions
Assumptions simplify the model and must be clearly stated.
What to include:
•	Service rules
•	Time distributions
•	System limitations
Example Assumptions:
•	Customer arrivals follow an exponential distribution
•	Service times are normally distributed
•	Only one cashier is initially available
•	Customers are served on a first-in, first-out (FIFO) basis
•	No customer leaves the queue once they enter
•	The system operates continuously during business hours
7. Simulation Setup
This section explains how the simulation is run.
What to include:
•	Run length
•	Number of replications
•	Performance measures
Example Explanation:
The simulation is run for an equivalent of 8 working hours per day. To account for randomness, 20 replications are performed. Performance measures collected include average waiting time, average queue length, number of customers served, and cashier utilization. These measures provide a reliable estimate of system performance.
8. Results & Analysis
This is the most important section.
What to include:
•	Tables or graphs
•	Interpretation of results
•	Comparison of scenarios
Example Explanation:
The simulation results show that the average customer waiting time is relatively high when only one cashier is available. Cashier utilization is close to maximum, indicating system congestion. When a second cashier is introduced, the average waiting time and queue length are significantly reduced. This demonstrates that resource availability has a strong impact on customer service performance.
9. Recommendations
Recommendations explain what actions should be taken based on results.
What to include:
•	Practical suggestions

Example Recommendations:
Based on the simulation results, it is recommended to add an additional cashier during peak hours to reduce customer waiting time. Alternatively, improving service efficiency through staff training or automated payment systems can also enhance system performance without increasing staffing costs.
