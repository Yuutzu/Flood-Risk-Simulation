================================================================================
1. INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

Jade Valley Subdivision is a residential community in Davao City, Philippines,
situated in a low-lying terrain that is highly susceptible to flooding during
heavy rainfall events and tropical cyclones. The area covers approximately
326.44 hectares with elevations ranging from 2.00 m to 71.75 m above mean
sea level, and a mean terrain elevation of 20.74 m. With 72.3% of the land
classified as flat (slope < 5°), surface runoff accumulates rapidly during
storm events, making the subdivision vulnerable to both pluvial flooding from
direct rainfall and fluvial flooding from nearby river overflow.

This system is an interactive, terrain-aware flood simulation developed as a
final project to model flood behavior across Jade Valley Subdivision under
various storm scenarios, from light rain to severe typhoon conditions. The
simulation is built entirely in Python using rasterio, NumPy, SciPy, and
Matplotlib, and operates on a GeoTIFF Digital Elevation Model (DEM) derived
from 3D topographic survey data of the subdivision.

The system is structured across three progressive simulation builds:

  - flood_simulation_25%.py  — Baseline flood model with no mitigation
  - flood_simulation_50%.py  — Baseline + 2 prevention measures
                               (Riverbank Floodwall, Drainage Canal Network)
  - flood_simulation_75%.py  — Baseline + 4 prevention measures
                               (Floodwall, Canal, Retention Basin,
                               Elevated Emergency Road)

Each build is self-contained, fully interactive, and outputs animated
visualizations plus time-series CSV data for further analysis.


================================================================================
2. PROBLEM DEFINITION
================================================================================

Davao City, located in Mindanao, Philippines, is among the most flood-prone
urban centers in the country due to its proximity to river systems, high annual
rainfall, and rapid urbanization of low-lying areas. Jade Valley Subdivision,
a residential community within the city, exemplifies the compounding flood risk
faced by such communities: flat terrain (72.3% of land at slope < 5°) limits
natural drainage, while proximity to river channels exposes residents to both
surface runoff accumulation and fluvial overflow during storm events.

Despite these conditions, flood risk planning in residential subdivisions often
lacks site-specific quantitative modeling. Without a terrain-accurate simulation,
it is difficult to answer critical questions such as:

  - Which specific zones within the subdivision flood first, and at what
    rainfall threshold does inundation become critical?

  - How far does river overflow spread across the subdivision during typhoon
    events of varying intensities, and what is the expected water depth?

  - How much flood extent and peak depth reduction can be achieved by
    constructing physical flood prevention infrastructure — and which
    combination of measures provides the best protection?

  - Which areas can safely serve as evacuation zones during a 100-year
    flood event?

The absence of a dynamic, interactive flood model specific to Jade Valley
Subdivision means that emergency preparedness decisions, infrastructure
investments, and land use recommendations are made without direct evidence
of how water moves across the terrain during actual storm conditions.

This simulation system addresses that gap by providing a terrain-aware,
scenario-driven flood model grounded in standard hydrological methods.
It enables planners, researchers, and local stakeholders to visualize flood
progression, evaluate the measurable impact of prevention measures, and
identify risk zones with spatial precision — all derived from the actual
topographic data of Jade Valley Subdivision.

================================================================================
3. SIMULATION OBJECTIVES
================================================================================

The simulation system is designed to meet the following objectives:

  3.1  Terrain-Accurate Flood Inundation Modeling
       Model realistic flood inundation behavior over the Jade Valley terrain
       using physically-grounded hydrological methods (D8 flow routing,
       Green-Ampt infiltration, BFS overflow dilation).

  3.2  Multi-Scenario Storm Coverage
       Simulate a range of PAGASA-calibrated storm scenarios — from Light Rain
       (15 mm / 2 h) to Typhoon Signal 3 (400 mm / 18 h) — so that flood
       risk can be assessed across multiple return periods.

  3.3  Flood Risk Quantification and Zone Classification
       Quantify flood risk across the subdivision using the HAND (Height Above
       Nearest Drainage) model and classify land into five risk zones:
       Very High, High, Medium, Low, and Safe.

  3.4  Prevention Measure Effectiveness Evaluation
       Evaluate the effectiveness of four flood prevention infrastructure
       measures — Riverbank Floodwall, Drainage Canal Network, Retention Basin,
       and Elevated Emergency Road — by running a parallel baseline simulation
       and computing improvement deltas frame-by-frame.

  3.5  Interactive Real-Time Visualization
       Provide an interactive animated interface so that stakeholders can
       visually observe flood progression, peak inundation areas, and the
       impact of prevention measures in real time.

  3.6  Data Export for Planning and Decision-Making
       Export simulation results as GIF animations, time-series CSV files,
       and static risk maps to support planning and disaster preparedness
       decision-making.

4. SYSTEM DESCRIPTION
================================================================================

The Jade Valley Flood Simulation is a Python-based desktop application that
combines terrain analysis, hydrological modeling, and interactive visualization
into a single, self-contained system. It is organized into three progressive
build levels, each adding physical flood prevention infrastructure on top of
the previous baseline.

  ── 4.1  Architecture Overview  ──────────────────────────────────────────────

  The system follows a linear pipeline architecture with five logical layers:

    [1] Data Layer
          Reads the GeoTIFF DEM and JPEG satellite background from disk.
          All terrain computations derive from the 57×61 elevation grid.

    [2] Input Layer
          A Tkinter GUI collects storm parameters and prevention measure
          settings from the user before the simulation begins.

    [3] Preprocessing Layer
          Applies prevention measure DEM modifications, then runs the full
          hydrological preprocessing pipeline (depression fill, D8 flow
          direction, flow accumulation, slope, runoff coefficient,
          infiltration capacity, bank elevation map).

    [4] Simulation Engine
          A frame-by-frame physics loop advances the flood state over time
          using six operations per timestep: rainfall addition, water routing,
          river overflow via BFS dilation, infiltration, and drainage.
          A parallel baseline simulation runs simultaneously when prevention
          measures are active, enabling direct comparison.

    [5] Output Layer
          Matplotlib renders a multi-layer animated visualization with a
          live statistics panel and interactive playback controls. Results
          are exportable as GIF animations and CSV time-series files.

  ── 4.2  Build Levels  ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Build           Script                         Prevention Measures
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  25% Baseline    flood_simulation_25%.py         None
                  Pure hydrological simulation; establishes the unmitigated
                  flood behavior as the reference for comparison.

  50% Build       flood_simulation_50%.py         2 measures
                  Adds Riverbank Floodwall and Drainage Canal Network.
                  Runs improved and baseline simulations in parallel and
                  displays improvement deltas in the stats panel.

  75% Build       flood_simulation_75%.py         4 measures
                  Adds Retention Basin and Elevated Emergency Road on top
                  of the 50% measures. Renders all four infrastructure
                  overlays simultaneously on the animated map.

  ── 4.3  Core Components  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Component                   Location / Role
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  load_dem()                  Reads GeoTIFF, fills NoData, returns (dem, cellsize)
  _fill_depressions()         Priority-Flood sink removal
  _d8_flow_direction()        Steepest-descent D8 routing
  _flow_accumulation()        Topological-sort upstream count
  build_stream_mask()         Identifies top-8% accumulation as river channel
  apply_floodwall()           DEM modification — raises western bank cells
  apply_drainage_canal()      DEM modification — lowers east/south corridors
  apply_retention_basin()     DEM modification — excavates basin depression [75%]
  apply_elevated_road()       DEM modification — raises emergency road berm [75%]
  apply_prevention_measures() Combines all active measures into one modified DEM
  FloodSimulation             Class — holds state, runs physics per timestep
    .add_rainfall()             Applies runoff-weighted rainfall to grid
    .route_water()              D8 diffusion of surface water (12 sub-iters)
    .apply_river_overflow()     BFS dilation of flood front from river channel
    .apply_infiltration()       Green-Ampt inspired soil absorption
    .apply_drainage()           Channel and canal drainage removal
    .step()                     Calls all five methods for one timestep
  wind_rainfall_map()         Generates wind-modified spatial rainfall weights
  run_simulation()            Orchestrates preprocessing, loop, and rendering
  SimulationGUI               Tkinter two-tab GUI for user parameter input

5. SYSTEM FLOW
================================================================================

The system executes in eight major stages from startup to output:

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  STAGE 1 — Data Loading
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  The script begins by loading the primary terrain data:

    a) GeoTIFF DEM (JVS_Simulation.tif) loaded via rasterio
         - Elevation read as float64 array (57 rows x 61 cols)
         - NoData pixels replaced with nearest valid elevation (scipy EDT)
         - Cell size derived in metres, corrected for geographic CRS via
           cosine latitude scaling

    b) JPEG satellite background (JVS_2D.jpg) loaded via Pillow
         - Resized to max 2048 px on longest side for performance
         - Used as the base visual layer in the animation

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  STAGE 2 — User Input via Tkinter GUI
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  A two-tab Tkinter GUI is presented to the user before the simulation runs:

    Tab 1 — Storm Scenario
      - Select preset storm (Scenarios 1–6) or Custom (Scenario 7)
      - Sliders: Rainfall (mm), Duration (h), Timestep (min)
      - Sliders: Wind Speed (km/h), Wind Direction (degrees)
      - Sliders: Soil Saturation (%), Drainage Capacity (mm/hr)
      - Rainfall Pattern selector: uniform / progressive / burst / decreasing
      - Storm start time field (HH:MM)

    Tab 2 — Prevention Measures (50% / 75% builds only)
      - Toggle: Riverbank Floodwall  +  Wall Height slider (m)
      - Toggle: Drainage Canal       +  Canal Depth slider (m)
      - Toggle: Retention Basin      +  Basin Depth slider (m)   [75% only]
      - Toggle: Elevated Emergency Road + Road Height slider (m)  [75% only]

  On pressing RUN, the GUI closes and passes all parameters to run_simulation().

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  STAGE 3 — DEM Modification (Prevention Measures)
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  When any prevention measure is enabled, the original DEM is modified
  BEFORE the simulation begins. Each measure physically alters elevation values
  so that water genuinely responds to the infrastructure in the simulation:

    Measure 1 — Riverbank Floodwall
      Target: cells with elevation ≤ 6 m within 3-cell dilation of river
      Action: raises those cells by wall_height_m (default 1.5 m)
      Effect: blocks river overflow until water surface exceeds wall crest

    Measure 2 — Drainage Canal Network
      Canal A: lowers east-running corridor from row of lowest mean elevation
               to eastern grid boundary (depth: default 2.0 m, min floor 0.3 m)
      Canal B: lowers south-running corridor from peak flow accumulation column
               to the southern boundary
      Effect: provides fast drainage paths out of the residential core

    Measure 3 — Retention Basin  [75% only]
      Target: centroid of highest flow accumulation zone outside river channel
      Action: excavates a circular depression covering 6% of grid area
              to depth of (dem_cell − 6.0 m), minimum floor 0.5 m
      Effect: stores peak runoff; reduces inundation depth downstream

    Measure 4 — Elevated Emergency Road  [75% only]
      Target: east-west corridor at ~45% row from grid top, skipping river
      Action: raises corridor cells by road_height_m (default 1.5 m)
      Effect: acts as a cross-subdivision berm; keeps emergency access dry

  The original DEM is preserved as a pristine copy for the parallel baseline run.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  STAGE 4 — Hydrological Preprocessing
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Executed on the modified DEM (improved run) and separately on the original
  DEM (baseline run):

    a) Depression Filling — Priority-Flood algorithm (Barnes et al. 2014)
         Removes artificial terrain sinks so flow paths are continuous.

    b) D8 Flow Direction
         Each cell is assigned to the steepest of its 8 neighbours.
         Diagonal neighbours use Euclidean distance (sqrt(cell_w^2 + cell_h^2)).

    c) Flow Accumulation — topological sort (high elevation to low)
         Counts the total number of upstream cells draining into each cell.

    d) Stream / River Mask
         Cells in the top 8% of flow accumulation are classified as
         active river channel cells.

    e) Slope Computation
         Computed as the gradient magnitude via numpy.gradient.

    f) Runoff Coefficient (per cell)
         runoff_coeff = clip(0.12 + 0.18 * slope_norm + 0.12 * scenario_factor,
                             0.12, 0.85)
         Higher slopes and more severe scenarios produce more surface runoff.

    g) Infiltration Capacity (per cell)
         max_inf = 1.2 + 1.2 * elev_norm * (1 - initial_saturation)
                       - 0.7 * scenario_factor
         Green-Ampt inspired; lower in high-saturation and low-elevation cells.

    h) Bank Elevation Map
         For each river cell, the minimum elevation among non-river neighbours
         is recorded as the bank crest. The median of all valid bank crests
         sets the base water surface elevation (flood_base_wse) for overflow.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  STAGE 5 — Per-Timestep Simulation Loop
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  The loop runs for num_frames = ceil(duration_h / timestep_h) iterations.
  At each timestep the following five physics operations execute in order:

    Step 1 — Rainfall Intensity Factor
      Scales the rainfall rate for this timestep by the storm pattern:
        uniform     : constant 1.0 across all frames
        progressive : ramps from 0.40 to 1.40 (storm development)
        burst       : Gaussian peak centred at 45% of duration (0.30–1.80)
        decreasing  : heavy start tapering to 0.20 (convective event)

    Step 2 — add_rainfall()
      rain_water += (rate_mmhr * dt_h * intensity / 1000) * runoff_coeff
                    * wind_map
      Wind map modifies spatial distribution of rainfall based on wind
      speed and direction relative to terrain slope aspect.

    Step 3 — route_water()  [12 sub-iterations]
      D8 water surface diffusion over 8 neighbours.
      Transfer fraction (tf) = clip(0.03 + 0.22 * slope_norm, 0.03, 0.35)
      Flow capped at 20% of source cell's rain_water per sub-iteration.
      Floodwall cells block cross-wall transfers unless water surface
      exceeds wall crest by more than 2 cm.

    Step 4 — apply_river_overflow()
      - River level raised by rain_m * rise_multiplier
      - rise_multiplier scales with rainfall accumulated and scenario severity
      - Overflow suppressed below 30 mm accumulated rainfall
      - Floodwall prevents overflow until river level > wall crest + 2 cm
      - BFS dilation expands flood front into cells where terrain < effective
        water surface elevation (WSE)
        Hops: 1 (light) → 2 (moderate) → 2–4 (heavy) → 4 (typhoon)
      - river_water updated in flooded land cells
      - Canal cells receive 40% less river_water (drainage effect)

    Step 5 — apply_infiltration()
      Saturation increases each step:
        sat_inc = min(0.12 * dt_h, 1 - saturation) * (0.5 + 0.5 * (1 - elev_norm))
      Infiltration removes water:
        inf_m = max_inf * (1 - saturation) / 1000 * dt_h
        rain_water  -= inf_m
        river_water -= inf_m * 0.5

    Step 6 — apply_drainage()
      Base rate: drainage_capacity * (1 - 0.45 * scenario_factor)
      Channel cells drain at base + 80 * flow_weight^2 mm/hr
      Canal cells drain 30x faster than base rate
      Very shallow water (< 3–4 mm) decays at 60–65% per step

    After all steps: snapshots of rain_water and river_water arrays are
    appended to the frame list along with statistics.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  STAGE 6 — Parallel Baseline Run (50% / 75% with prevention active)
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  When any prevention measure is enabled, an identical simulation is run on the
  unmodified DEM with no prevention measures. Its flooded_pct and max_depth_mm
  are stored per frame so the stats panel can display improvement deltas.

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  STAGE 7 — Animation Rendering (Matplotlib)
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Five visual layers are composited per frame:

    Layer 1 — JPEG satellite background (fallback: greyscale elevation shading)
    Layer 2 — River channel band (blue tint overlay on stream cells)
    Layer 3 — Rain accumulation depth (transparent cyan → orange → red)
    Layer 4 — River overflow depth (transparent → solid dark blue)
    Layer 5 — Infrastructure overlays:
               Red    — Riverbank Floodwall cells
               Cyan   — Drainage Canal cells
               Green  — Retention Basin cells  [75% only]
               Orange — Elevated Emergency Road cells  [75% only]

  Live stats panel displays per frame:
    - Accumulated rainfall (mm)
    - Flooded area (% of grid)
    - River overflow area (%)
    - Maximum water depth (mm)
    - Baseline vs. improved delta  (when prevention is active)

  Playback controls: Play/Pause  |  Frame scrubber  |  Speed slider  |
                     Step forward / Step backward

  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  STAGE 8 — Output Export
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Results/animations/
      [scenario]_[timestamp].gif         — exported animation (all frames)

    Results/data/
      sim_[scenario]_BASELINE.csv        — time-series: time, rain_mm,
                                           flooded_pct, river_pct, max_depth_mm
      sim_[scenario]_WITH_PREVENTION.csv — same, for prevention run
      dem_processed.npy                  — processed DEM array
      flow_accumulation.npy              — flow accumulation grid
      hand_model.npy                     — HAND values per cell
      slope.npy                          — slope magnitude grid
      risk_map.npy                       — 5-class risk zone grid
      flood_risk_report.txt              — HAND-based risk assessment report
      flood_statistics.json              — return-period scenario statistics

    Results/maps/
      [static risk zone and flood maps]

7. ASSUMPTIONS
================================================================================

The following assumptions and simplifications govern the simulation model:

  5.1  Single-Direction Flow (D8)
       Each terrain cell routes water to exactly one of its eight neighbours
       (the steepest descent). Real flow may divide across multiple paths;
       this is an accepted simplification standard in raster hydrology.

  5.2  Static DEM
       The DEM does not change during the simulation. Erosion, sedimentation,
       and structural collapse are not modeled. Prevention measure modifications
       are applied once before the simulation starts and remain fixed.

  5.3  Uniform Soil Properties
       Initial soil saturation is spatially uniform (user-specified %). The
       infiltration capacity model uses elevation as a proxy for soil drainage
       class. In reality, Davao City soils vary spatially in texture, porosity,
       and hydraulic conductivity.

  5.4  Green-Ampt Simplified Infiltration
       The infiltration model is inspired by Green-Ampt but is a simplified
       scalar approximation. Capillary suction head, wetting front, and
       detailed soil hydraulics are not explicitly computed.

  5.5  BFS River Overflow Approximation
       River overflow spreading uses BFS (Breadth-First Search) dilation rather
       than a full shallow-water equation solver. This is computationally
       efficient and visually representative but does not model wave celerity,
       backwater effects, or momentum.

  5.6  Instantaneous Prevention Measure Deployment
       All prevention measures are fully constructed and effective from
       timestep 0. Construction time, soil settlement, and partial effectiveness
       during construction are not modeled.

  5.7  No Groundwater Interaction
       Subsurface flow, groundwater table rise, and sewer system interactions
       are not included. The model is a surface hydrology simulation only.

  5.8  Wind Uniformity
       Wind speed and direction are assumed constant throughout the storm
       duration. Spatial variation in wind field is not modeled.

  5.9  PAGASA Rainfall Classification
       Storm scenarios are calibrated to PAGASA (Philippine Atmospheric,
       Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration) rainfall intensity
       classifications as applied to Mindanao / Davao City conditions.

  5.10 Retention Basin Instantaneous Fill
       The retention basin stores water immediately as it is excavated in the
       DEM. Real basin effectiveness depends on inlet design, spillways, and
       outlet control structures which are not explicitly modeled.

  5.11 Grid Resolution
       The DEM grid is 57 x 61 cells at ~30.64 m per cell. Features smaller
       than one cell (roads, buildings, drainage pipes) are sub-grid and
       their effects are approximated through the runoff coefficient and
       drainage capacity parameters.

9. CONCLUSION
================================================================================

The Jade Valley Flood Simulation system successfully models the hydrological
behavior of a real-world residential subdivision in Davao City under a full
spectrum of storm conditions relevant to the Philippine context. By combining
D8 terrain-based flow routing, scenario-adaptive river overflow via BFS
dilation, and Green-Ampt inspired infiltration, the simulation captures the
essential dynamics of urban flooding at a computationally accessible scale.

Key findings from the pre-computed HAND risk assessment confirm that the
subdivision faces genuine flood risk:

  - 8.2%  of the area (26.66 ha) is classified Very High Risk
    and will flood under Heavy Rain (≥ 90 mm) conditions.
  - 9.7%  (31.83 ha) is High Risk, flooding at Typhoon Signal 1 (≥ 150 mm).
  - 9.9%  (32.30 ha) is Medium Risk, flooding at Typhoon Signal 2 (≥ 250 mm).
  - 11.0% (35.86 ha) is Low Risk, flooding only in extreme Sig 3 events.
  - 53.6% (174.81 ha) is classified as Safe Zone, suitable for use as an
    evacuation destination during flood events.

Return-period analysis shows that a 100-year flood event would inundate
35.5% (115.76 ha) of the subdivision to a mean depth of 4.12 m, underscoring
the severity of the risk and the importance of mitigation infrastructure.

The three-build simulation architecture allows direct, frame-by-frame
comparison of flood extent and depth with and without prevention measures.
The results demonstrate that:

  - The Riverbank Floodwall effectively delays and reduces river overflow into
    the residential core by requiring water to overtop the raised crest before
    spreading onto adjacent land.

  - The Drainage Canal Network accelerates runoff evacuation from the
    flood-prone central zone, reducing peak rain_water accumulation in areas
    that would otherwise be slow to drain.

  - The Retention Basin (75% build) provides the most significant reduction
    in peak inundation depth by storing excess floodwater at the subdivision's
    primary topographic sink before it spreads over residential land.

  - The Elevated Emergency Road (75% build) maintains critical cross-
    subdivision access for emergency vehicles even at design flood level,
    while its berm effect deflects surface flow laterally rather than allowing
    it to pool freely in the central zone.

Together, the four prevention measures in the 75% build represent a layered
flood risk reduction strategy aligned with the nature of the terrain and the
hydrology of the site. This simulation provides a quantitative and visual
basis for infrastructure planning, community disaster preparedness, and
policy recommendation for flood-vulnerable residential areas such as
Jade Valley Subdivision.
