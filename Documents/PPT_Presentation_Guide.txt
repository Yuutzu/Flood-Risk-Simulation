  PURPOSE OF THIS SECTION

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  SLIDE CONTENT
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Paragraph — Study Area and Context:

    Jade Valley Subdivision is a residential community located in Davao City,
    Mindanao, Philippines. It occupies approximately 326.44 hectares of
    predominantly flat, low-lying terrain, with elevations ranging from 2.00 m
    to 71.75 m above mean sea level and a mean terrain elevation of 20.74 m.
    Over 72.3% of the land has a slope of less than 5°, classifying the majority
    of the site as flat. This geophysical characteristic severely limits the
    natural drainage capacity of the terrain — during heavy rainfall events,
    surface runoff cannot disperse quickly and instead accumulates across the
    residential area. Combined with the subdivision's proximity to a river
    channel, the site is exposed to two simultaneous flood mechanisms: pluvial
    flooding from direct rainfall accumulation and fluvial flooding from river
    overflow into adjacent low-lying land.

  Paragraph — The Simulation System:

    To quantify and visualize this flood risk, this study developed an interactive,
    terrain-aware flood simulation system built entirely in Python, using NumPy,
    SciPy, rasterio, and Matplotlib. The system operates on a GeoTIFF Digital
    Elevation Model (DEM) derived from actual 3D topographic survey data of
    Jade Valley Subdivision, ensuring that all modeled flood behavior is grounded
    in the real terrain geometry of the site. The simulation models how rainfall
    accumulates, routes across the terrain, infiltrates into the soil, and
    overflows from river channels — frame by frame, timestep by timestep — across
    a range of storm scenarios from light rain to severe typhoon conditions.

  Bullets — Three Progressive Build Levels:

    — 25% Baseline Build
      A pure hydrological simulation with no flood prevention measures. Establishes
      the unmitigated flood behavior of the terrain as a reference for comparison.

    — 50% Build
      Adds two flood prevention measures on top of the baseline: a Riverbank
      Floodwall and a Drainage Canal Network. Runs a parallel baseline to
      quantify the improvement frame by frame.

    — 75% Build
      Adds two additional measures on top of the 50% build: a Retention Basin
      and an Elevated Emergency Road. Represents the most comprehensive flood
      mitigation scenario evaluated in this study.

  Pipeline Table (two columns: Layer Name | What It Does):

                     | and prevention measure settings before simulation runs.
  ───────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  SECTION 5 — MODEL DESIGN  (Slide 6)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

  PURPOSE OF THIS SECTION
  ────────────────────────
  Explain the physics behind the simulation — what happens at each timestep
  and how storm scenarios and prevention measures are parameterized.

  SLIDE CONTENT
  ─────────────
  Left Side — Per-Timestep Physics (5 operations):

  1. Rainfall Addition
     Applies a rainfall intensity factor (uniform / progressive / burst /
     decreasing) and adds rainfall to each cell weighted by its runoff
     coefficient and a wind-direction spatial map.

  2. Water Routing
     D8 diffusion distributes surface water across 8 neighbours in 12
     sub-iterations per timestep. Transfer fraction is 0.03–0.35 based
     on local slope, capped at 20% of the source cell's water per sub-iter.

  3. River Overflow (BFS)
     River water level rises each step proportionally to accumulated rainfall.
     A Breadth-First Search dilation expands the flood front into adjacent
     cells whose terrain elevation falls below the current water surface.

  4. Infiltration
     Soil saturation increases each step. A Green-Ampt-inspired formula
     removes water at a rate proportional to remaining soil capacity.

  5. Drainage
     Channel cells drain faster (base + 80×flow_weight² mm/hr).
     Canal cells drain 30× faster. Very shallow residual water (< 4 mm)
     decays at 60–65% per step.

  Right Side — Storm Scenarios Table:
  ┌────────────────────┬──────────┬──────────┬────────────┐
  │ Scenario           │ Rainfall │ Duration │ Pattern    │
  ├────────────────────┼──────────┼──────────┼────────────┤
  │ Light Rain         │  15 mm   │  2.0 h   │ Uniform    │
  │ Moderate Rain      │  36 mm   │  3.0 h   │ Progressive│
  │ Heavy Rain         │  90 mm   │  4.0 h   │ Progressive│
  │ Typhoon Signal 1   │ 150 mm   │  8.0 h   │ Progressive│
  │ Typhoon Signal 2   │ 250 mm   │ 12.0 h   │ Burst      │
  │ Typhoon Signal 3   │ 400 mm   │ 18.0 h   │ Burst      │
  │ Custom             │ User     │ User     │ User       │
  └────────────────────┴──────────┴──────────┴────────────┘

  Right Side — Prevention Measures Table:
  ┌────────────────────────┬──────────────┬──────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Measure                │ Build        │ Mechanism                        │
  ├────────────────────────┼──────────────┼──────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Riverbank Floodwall    │ 50% + 75%    │ Raises river bank cells          │
  │ Drainage Canal Network │ 50% + 75%    │ 30× faster drainage corridor     │
  │ Retention Basin        │ 75% only     │ Excavates depression at flow sink │
  │ Elevated Emergency Road│ 75% only     │ Raises E-W berm; deflects flow   │
  └────────────────────────┴──────────────┴──────────────────────────────────┘

  TALKING POINTS
  ───────────────
  "At every timestep the simulation performs five operations in sequence. First,
  it adds rainfall to the grid based on the storm's intensity pattern. Second,
  it routes that water downhill using the D8 algorithm — water moves toward the
  steepest neighbor. Third, the river channel level rises and BFS dilation spreads
  overflow into adjacent low-lying land. Fourth, soil absorbs some water through
  an infiltration formula. Fifth, drainage removes water through channels.

  The storm scenarios range from 15 mm of light rain to 400 mm in a severe
  typhoon — that is a 26-fold increase in rainfall over the scenario range.
  The four prevention measures each work by physically modifying the elevation
  model before the simulation starts, so the physics responds to them naturally."

  KEY POINTS TO HIGHLIGHT
  ────────────────────────
  — 5 ordered physics operations execute every timestep — no shortcuts
  — D8 routing uses 12 sub-iterations for stability on shallow terrain
  — BFS overflow dilation is terrain-accurate: it only floods cells below
    the current water surface elevation
  — Storm scenarios cover 15 mm (light) to 400 mm (typhoon) = full Davao spectrum
  — Prevention measures modify the DEM directly — physics responds genuinely

     Community drills and education programs should familiarize residents with

  GENERAL PRESENTATION TIPS
================================================================================

  1. FLOW OF ARGUMENT
     The sections are ordered logically: context (Intro) → problem (Problem) →
     what we set out to do (Objectives) → how the system works (System + Model) →
     what we assumed (Assumptions) → how to operate it (Setup) → what we found
     (Results) → what should be done (Recommendations). Follow this flow naturally
     in your delivery.

  2. DATA-FIRST MINDSET FOR RESULTS
     When presenting Section 8 (Results), lead with numbers first, then interpret.
     Example: "8.2 percent of the subdivision — that is 26.66 hectares — is Very
     High Risk" → then explain what Very High Risk means and why it matters.

  3. AVOID READING SLIDES
     Slides contain the key content. Your job is to explain and connect the ideas,
     not read the text. Use the TALKING POINTS in this guide as your verbal script.

  4. ANTICIPATE QUESTIONS
     Common questions this audience may ask:
     — "How accurate is the D8 model compared to real floods?"
        → D8 is the standard in raster hydrology. For a 30 m grid, it is
          appropriate. A full 2D shallow-water model would require significantly
          more computational resources and calibration data we do not have.
     — "Was the simulation validated against real flood events?"
        → This is an academic study. We used real terrain data and storm scenarios
          calibrated to the local rainfall context. Validation against historical
          events would require gauge data that is outside the scope of this project.
     — "Why is the retention basin placed in the outer perimeter?"
        → It is placed where flow accumulation is highest in an area of open land,
          specifically to avoid any impact on existing residential structures.
     — "What is HAND and why use it?"
        → HAND (Height Above Nearest Drainage) measures how high each point of
          land sits above the nearest stream channel. Lower HAND values mean the
          land is closer in elevation to the river, so it floods sooner and deeper.
          It is a standard GIS-based flood risk classification method.
